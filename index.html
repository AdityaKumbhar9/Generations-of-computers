First Generation (1940s-1950s)

- Technology: Relied on vacuum tubes and magnetic drums.
- Characteristics: Large, expensive, unreliable, and consumed a lot of power.
- Examples: ENIAC, UNIVAC

Second Generation (1950s-1960s)

- Technology: Transistors replaced vacuum tubes; magnetic core memory was used.
- Characteristics: Smaller, faster, more reliable, and less power-hungry than first-generation computers.
- Examples: IBM 1401, IBM 7090.

Third Generation (1960s-1970s)

- Technology: Integrated Circuits (ICs) were introduced, allowing more components to be integrated onto a single chip.
- Characteristics: Further reduction in size, cost, and power consumption; increased computing power.
- Examples: DEC PDP-11, IBM System/360.

Fourth Generation (1970s-1980s)

- Technology: Microprocessors emerged, enabling the creation of smaller and cheaper computers.
- Characteristics: Personal computers became feasible, with improved graphical user interfaces and networking capabilities.
- Examples: IBM PC, Apple II, Commodore PET.

Fifth Generation (1980s-present)

- Technology: Very Large-Scale Integration (VLSI) allowed millions of transistors on a single chip.
- Characteristics: Rapid advancements in processing power, memory capacity, and connectivity; emergence of the internet and mobile computing.
- Examples: IBM AS/400, Intel Pentium series, Apple Macintosh.
